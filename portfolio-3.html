Injection Molded Ice Cream Cone

Description

  • Nate A, Christine H, Gretchen V, Gabe Z

  • January-March 2018

Northwestern desires to foster knowledge about engineering to young students in the nearby elementary and high schools. From a 4th graders' drawing, our team designed and manufactured an ice cream cone toy through injection molding.

Skills

  • Generated g-code of the cone to CNC the mold halves
  • Used Unigraphics NX for CAD and computer aided manufacturing (CAM) for the ice cream cone molds
  • Conducted production run for dimensional analysis

Defining the Problem

For the design process, this section was accelerated because the 4th grade students defined what toys our team made.

Conceptual Design

The team made some alterations to the orginal design to improve the manufacturability.

  • WAFFLE TO SUGAR: The cone switched from a waffle to sugar cone design so the plastic would not be injected to a point. The flat base would allow for the toy to stand.
  • HIERARCHY OF INSTRUCTIONS: Ideally, the user reads everything. However, in high stress situations, it is crucial to higlight the key points for the user.
  • PACKAGING: Pakcaging is crucial in effectively helping the user to perform initial medical care.

Andrew Webber, a West Point graduate and current NU law student, came to our team to have us design a wound kit that would be more financially and intuitively accessible to civilian first responders. The team decided to split the project into redesigning the tourniquet and the wound kit.

Manufacturing Operations

Through benchmark testing, our team noticed that the tourniquet needed to be strapped tightly around the limb before twisting to get sufficient pressure to stop blood flow. The team divided the tourniquet operation into three simple steps:

  1. PLACE
  2. PULL
  3. TWIST

For the wound kit, I led the team in brainstorming the packaging design for the wound kit to clearly and effectively instruct a civilian in high stress situatinos. Key design features are:

  • RED NUMBERS: The color pops out to help the civilian properly perform tasks.
  • A/B Variations: Depending on the wound, the civilian needs to perform different tasks.
  • Word Hierarchy: The smaller font indicates instructions that are less important but necessary.

Injection Molding

After 20 weeks of design iteration, the team presented these final products: the Trauma Wound Kit and T-bar Tourniquet.

Key takeaways from the project include:

  • TRAINING: Research and testing showed that trained users are more effective with the product than unstrained users
  • HIERARCHY OF INSTRUCTIONS: Ideally, the user reads everything. However, in high stress situations, it is crucial to higlight the key points for the user.
  • PACKAGING: Pakcaging is crucial in effectively helping the user to perform initial medical care.
